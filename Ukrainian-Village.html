Ukrainian Village

History

Christ Woehsner, the mayor of Cudahy, Wisconsin, thought a public park ought to be constructed alongside Lake Michigan in 1913. He expressed his heartfelt gratitude for the region's beauty in a letter, [clarification needed]. The letter resulted in the proprietor, Patrick Cudahy, selling his property close to Lake Michigan. The Cudahy Public Park was recognized as a park in 1914. To honor Major-General Philip Henry Sheridan, the name was changed to Sheridan Park in 1920.

Churches

Ukrainian Village is home to a number of churches, the majority of which are within a block of one another and three of which are situated on a single street. The first of the village's two Ukrainian Greek Catholic churches was St. Nicholas Ukrainian Catholic Cathedral. In the 1970s, Ss. Volodymyr and Olha Ukrainian Catholic Church was founded as a result of a disagreement within St. Nicholas' parish. The fourth wave of Ukrainian immigrants from the 1990s are predominantly served by St. Volodymyr Ukrainian Orthodox Cathedral, which represents the Eastern European Ukrainian Orthodox Church. The first Eastern European Russian Orthodox church to be constructed in what was then known as the "Slavic Village" was Holy Trinity Russian Orthodox Cathedral. It was created by renowned architect Louis Sullivan on St. John Kochurov's behalf.

Ukrainian Catholic Cathedral of St. Nicholas
Immigrants from western and Carpathian Ukraine arrived in Chicago's north side in the late 1890s, marking the beginning of the country's Ukrainian history. They referred to themselves at the time as Rusyns (Ruthenians), an archaic national monicker connected to Ukraine's position within the Austro-Hungarian empire. The renowned Lysenko Chorus, which took first place in a 1930 multi-state choral competition sponsored by the Chicago Tribune, was from St. Nicholas.

Saints Volodymyr and Olha Ukrainian Catholic Church
Saints Volodymyr and Olha Ukrainian Catholic Church The Patriarch Josyf Slipyj and the bishop of the Eparchy of Chicago, Yaroslav Gabro, established the Saints Volodymyr and Olha Ukrainian Catholic Parish in Chicago in 1968. Together with the congregation, they wished to protect and uphold the Ukrainian Church's traditions. This church has a distinctive spiritual background, follows the Julian Calendar, and uses a traditional liturgy.

Ukrainian Orthodox Cathedral of St. Volodymyr
The primary religion in Ukraine is Eastern Orthodox Christianity, which is represented by the St. Volodymyr Ukrainian Orthodox Cathedral. Since the collapse of the Soviet Union, it has helped brand-new immigrants who have emigrated from Ukraine. Despite being of the Orthodox faith, the cathedral is governed by the UOCUSA, which falls within the Ecumenical Patriarchate's purview and is not connected to the Ukrainian Orthodox Church in Ukraine.

The chapel was initially constructed for a German congregation. Before the present Ukrainian parish ultimately purchased it and converted it, it had already been sold twice. The church offers a Saturday Ukrainian language school for children in preschool through tenth grade.

Ukrainian Village District

On December 4, 2002, the Ukrainian Village District, located in the area between Damen and Leavitt Avenues and centered on Haddon Avenue, Thomas Street, and Cortez Street, as well as a portion of Damen, Hoyne, and Leavitt Avenues, was named a Chicago Landmark District.Extensions to the district were formally established on April 11, 2007, and in 2005.
