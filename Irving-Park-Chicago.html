Irving Park, Chicago

About


On Chicago's Northwest Side, Irving Park is one of 77 legally recognized community zones. East of Pulaski Road, it extends to include the area between Belmont Avenue on the south and, roughly, Leland Avenue on the north. It is bordered by the Chicago River on the east, the Milwaukee Road train tracks on the west, Addison Street on the south, and Montrose Avenue on the north. It bears the name Washington Irving after the American author.

Old Irving Park, which is bordered by Cicero Avenue, Addison Street, Pulaski Road, and Montrose Avenue, has a diverse housing stock that includes Queen Anne, Victorian, and Italianate residences, as well as a few farmhouses and lots of bungalows.

This area is traversed by the CTA Blue Line, which makes stops at Addison, Irving Park, and Montrose.


History


The construction of Irving Park started in 1843 when Major Noble bought a 160-acre (65 ha) plot of land from Christopher J. Ward and built a farm there. The current Montrose Avenue to the north, Irving Park Road to the south, Pulaski Road to the east, and Kostner Avenue to the west limits of that farm. The Buckthorn Tavern, which served visitors traveling to and from the city of Chicago along the North West Plank Road, was a part of Major Noble's home, which was located on the east side of Elston, just south of Montrose (Elston). Noble sold the farm after many years of prosperous farming and moved to McHenry County to retire. The farm was bought in 1869 for $20,000 USD by four New Yorkers: Charles T. Race, John S. Brown, Adelbert E. Brown, and John Wheeler. Soon after, for US$25,000, they paid John Gray for an additional 80-acre (32 ha) parcel of land south of the Noble property. This area was a portion of his original 320-acre (130 ha) property and was flanked by Irving Park to the north, Grace to the south, Pulaski to the east, and Kostner to the west. The men intended to carry on farming, but after witnessing the success of newly developed suburban areas, they decided to divide their land and establish an exclusive community seven miles (11 km) outside of the city.

In exchange for the construction of a station, the Chicago and Northwestern Railroad agreed to let its trains stop in Irving Park. This was accomplished, and the station is still open and serving the neighborhood's residents. Washington Irving's name was originally picked as the name of the new suburb, but Irving Park was chosen once it was learned that another town in Illinois had already used the same name.

Between 1870 and 1874, the original builders all constructed enormous mansions along Irving Park Boulevard. With the exception of the Steven A. Race mansion, which was relocated before the turn of the century and is now located at 3945 N. Tripp Avenue, all of them have since been demolished. At 3812 N. Pulaski Road, a different early house that was constructed for Erastus Brown—the father of John and Adalbert—still stands, albeit significantly altered. The Great Chicago Fire of 1871, which could be seen from the cupolas of many nearby homes, brought in a new population that constructed a large number of distinctive but not overly ostentatious homes.

The Dutch Reformed Church and Society of Irving Park was built on the southeast corner of Keeler Avenue and Belle Plaine Avenue in 1872, becoming the first church in the neighborhood. For thirteen years, it remained the lone place of worship. According to plans created by renowned architect Elmer C. Jensen, the structure underwent a comprehensive renovation in 1908. The magnificent private mansion designed by Jensen in the Colonial Revival style in 1905 still stands on North Lowell Avenue in the Old Irving Park area. Congregations for the Episcopalians, Methodists, Disciples of Christ, Catholics, and Baptists had been founded by the turn of the century.


Old Irving Park


The historical center of Chicago's Irving Park Community Area is represented by the neighborhood of Old Irving Park. Its limits are those of the original two farms that were established here in the 1870s: Montrose to the north, Addison to the south, Pulaski Road to the east, and Cicero to the west.
